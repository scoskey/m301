\section{Systems of linear equations and vectors}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Based on ILA 5th, \S 2.1.

\begin{itemize}
\item What is the equation of a line? Students may think of several answers, but the \emph{standard form} is $ax+by=c$. Why is it standard? It treats the variables symmetrically.
\item In this course we will look at generalizations of this simple type of equation
\item First we will consider the case when we have more than two variables. For example $ax+by+cz=d$. This is not the equation of a line (what is it?). But due to its similarity we still call it a \emph{linear equation}.
\item Second we will consider the case when we have more than one equation. For example, $ax+by=c$; $dx+ey=f$. This is called a \emph{system of linear equations}.
\item Let's look at several examples of simple problems which can be modeled by a system of linear equations.
\item Example 1. Economics. The NYT reported that ``The Interview'' had two million online transactions, split between rentals and downloads. A rental costs \$6 and a download costs \$15. The total revenue was \$15 million. The NYT could not determine how many transactions were rentals and how many were sales. Can you help them?
\[\systeme{r+s=2\text{ million},6r+15s=15\text{ million}}
\]
\item Example 2. Flows. Vehicles driving in each segment of a one-way grid are counted during a period of one hour. The results are shown in the figure.
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[shorten <=2pt,shorten >=2pt,scale=1.5]
      \draw[->] (0,1) -- node{$520$} (1,1);
      \draw[->] (1,1) -- node{$w$} (2,1);
      \draw[->] (2,1) -- node{$600$} (3,1);

      \draw[<-] (0,2) -- node{$610$} (1,2);
      \draw[<-] (1,2) -- node{$y$} (2,2);
      \draw[<-] (2,2) -- node{$640$} (3,2);

      \draw[<-] (1,0) -- node{$480$} (1,1);
      \draw[<-] (1,1) -- node{$z$} (1,2);
      \draw[<-] (1,2) -- node{$450$} (1,3);

      \draw[->] (2,0) -- node{$390$} (2,1);
      \draw[->] (2,1) -- node{$x$} (2,2);
      \draw[->] (2,2) -- node{$310$} (2,3);
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{center}
  Assume that for each intersection, the number of cars entering it is the same as the number of cars leaving it. What are the values of the unknowns?
  \[\begin{cases}x+640=y+310\\y+450=z+610\\z+520=w+480\\w+390=x+600\end{cases}
  \]
\item Example 3. Trend lines. Given sampled data points, what is the equation of a trend line that best describes the overall behavior of the data? For example suppose you are measuring the change in a quantity over time. At time $t=2$ you measure $3$, at time $t=3$ you measure $5$, and at time $t=6$ you measure $6$.
  \[\systeme{49m+11b-57=0,11m+3b-14=0}
  \]
\item Before we study how to solve these types of systems, let us visualize what is going on. Consider the example system
  \[\systeme{x-2y=1,3x+2y=11}
  \]
\item The classical way to visualize this is by graphing the two lines and looking for the point where they intersect.
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
      \draw[gray!30,very thin,dashed] (-0.9,-0.9) grid (4.9,4.9);
      \draw[->,thick] (-1,0)--(5,0);
      \draw[->,thick] (0,-1)--(0,5);
      \draw[domain=0:4] plot (\x,.5*\x-.5);
      \draw[domain=1:4] plot (\x,11/2-3/2*\x);
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{center}
\item However, another revealing way to visualize this system is to consider it as a \emph{single system} with \emph{vector coefficients}.
  \[\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}x+\begin{bmatrix}-2\\2\end{bmatrix}y
    =\begin{bmatrix}1\\11\end{bmatrix}
  \]
\item Note that we write our vectors vertically instead of horizontally, as one often does in calc 3.
\item Recall that we scale vectors by lenghening and shortening them, and that we add vectors ``tail to tip''. Thus this system is asking, how many of the vector $(1,2)$ and how many of the vector $(-2,2)$ do we need to obtain $(1,11)$?

  (figure)
\item Either way the answer is $x=3$, $y=1$.
\item Note that geogebra can be extremely helpful to visualize the row picture! It also shows the effect of elimination on the system.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Activity for \S \thesection}

\begin{activity}
  \item Solve the three systems of linear equations from the first lecture. Use any method.
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item (costs) \systeme{r+s=2\text{ million},6r+15s=15\text{ million}}
    \item (traffic flow) $\begin{cases}x+640=y+310\\y+450=z+610\\z+520=w+480\\w+390=x+600\end{cases}$
    \item (least squares) \systeme{49m+11b-57=0,11m+3b-14=0}
  \end{enumerate}
  \item In your own words, describe a good method for systematically solving systems of linear equations (such as those in the previous problem).
  \item For the following system of linear equations, draw the ``row picture (classical)'' and the ``column picture (vectors)''. Then find the solution.
  \[\systeme{3x+2y=6,2x+3y=6}
  \]
\end{activity}
